    07 | Sankhya Theory of Evolution¬∂

    Prak·πõti is regarded as essentially dynamic. If motion were not inherent in Prak·πõti, it could not be given to it by any outside agency, and if motion once ceased in Prak·πõti, it could not reappear. Hence, Prak·πõti is always changing.

    • Even in dissolution, there is a homogeneous change in Prak·πõti when all three gu·πáas are in a state of equilibrium.
    • Evolution begins only when a heterogeneous change takes place, and rajas (the principle of activity) vibrates, making sattva and tamas vibrate. This disturbs the equilibrium.
    • Before evolution, sattva (manifestation) and rajas (activity) are held in check by tamas (non-manifestation and non-activity).
    • When rajas vibrates and activates the other two, the process of creation begins.
    • Creation is not a new creation but only a manifestation of what was formerly implicit.

    Characteristics of Evolution¬∂

    • Cyclic, not Linear: Evolution is not a continuous progress in one direction but involves alternating periods of evolution and dissolution in a cyclic order.
    • Teleological, not Mechanical: Evolution is purposive and takes place to serve the purpose of the Puru·π£a.
    • Prak·πõti, the gu·πáas, senses, mind, ego, intellect, and subtle body all constantly serve the end of the Puru·π£a.
    • This end is either worldly experience (bhoga) or liberation (apavarga).
    • Puru·π£a needs Prak·πõti for both enjoyment (sa·πÉsƒÅra) and liberation (kaivalya).
    • Evolution supplies objects for the Puru·π£a to enjoy and also works for his liberation by enabling him to discriminate between himself and Prak·πõti.

    The Problem of the Union of Puru·π£a and Prak·πõti¬∂

    The central question: How does evolution begin? It begins when rajas disturbs the equilibrium of the gu·πáas. But how is the equilibrium disturbed?

    • SƒÅ·πÖkhya fails to answer this question satisfactorily. The fundamental blunder of SƒÅ·πÖkhya is separating Prak·πõti and Puru·π£a as absolute and independent entities.
    • If Prak·πõti and Puru·π£a are absolutely separate, they can never unite. And if they cannot unite, evolution cannot take place.

    SƒÅ·πÖkhya's explanation is that the disturbance of equilibrium is made possible by the contact of Puru·π£a and Prak·πõti.

    • "Puru·π£a without Prak·πõti is lame, and Prak·πõti without Puru·π£a is blind."
    • Prak·πõti needs Puru·π£a to be known, seen, and enjoyed.
    • Puru·π£a needs Prak·πõti to enjoy and also to obtain liberation by discriminating between himself and Prak·πõti.
    • For creation, they must unite. This union of the inactive Puru·π£a and the non-intelligent Prak·πõti disturbs the equilibrium of the gu·πáas and leads to evolution.

    But how can two opposed and independent entities really come into contact?

    • To avoid this difficulty, SƒÅ·πÖkhya says there is no real contact, but only the proximity of the Puru·π£a to Prak·πõti is sufficient to disturb the equilibrium.
    • New problem: If the Puru·π£a is always near to Prak·πõti, evolution should never stop, and dissolution would become impossible. Evolution would be beginningless, and the concept of Prak·πõti as a state of equilibrium would be impossible.

    SƒÅ·πÖkhya finds itself in a dilemma: either no contact and hence no evolution, or else no equilibrium and hence no Prak·πõti and no dissolution.

    • To avoid these difficulties, SƒÅ·πÖkhya posits the theory of a semblance of a contact.
    • There is no real contact; it is this semblance that leads to evolution.
    • Puru·π£a is reflected in the intellect (buddhi) and wrongly identifies himself with his own reflection.
    • It is this reflection of the Puru·π£a that comes into contact with Prak·πõti, not the Puru·π£a himself.
    • New problem: Buddhi (or mahat) is regarded as the first evolute of Prak·πõti. How can it arise before evolution to receive the reflection of the Puru·π£a?
    • If it is said that the Puru·π£a is reflected in Prak·πõti itself, then liberation and dissolution would become impossible because Prak·πõti is always there, and the Puru·π£a would never get liberated.
    • If a semblance of a contact is sufficient to disturb the equilibrium, then evolution itself becomes a semblance of evolution‚Äîan appearance only (vivarta) and not a real transformation (pari·πáƒÅma) of Prak·πõti.

    Thus, in order to defend the initial blunder of regarding Puru·π£a and Prak·πõti as absolute and independent entities, SƒÅ·πÖkhya commits blunder after blunder.

    The Evolutes¬∂

    1. Mahat (The Great):

      • The first product of evolution. It is the germ of the vast world of objects, including intellect, ego, and mind.
      • It is cosmic in nature.
      • Its psychological aspect is called buddhi or intellect.
      • Buddhi is distinguished from consciousness. Puru·π£a alone is pure consciousness. Buddhi, being an evolute of Prak·πõti, is material but made of the finest matter, capable of reflecting the consciousness of the Puru·π£a.
      • Its original attributes (when sattva predominates) are virtue (dharma), knowledge (j√±ƒÅna), detachment (vairƒÅgya), and power (ai≈õvarya). When vitiated by tamas, these are replaced by their opposites.
