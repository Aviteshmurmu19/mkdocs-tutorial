    So, what is inference? It's just using logic to figure something out that you haven't directly seen.

    The classic example they use in all Indian philosophy is this:

    You look at a distant hill. You see smoke rising from it. You don't see the fire, but you infer that there must be fire on the hill.

    This seems like simple, obvious logic, right? We do this a thousand times a day. Your phone is not in your pocket, so you infer you left it on the table.

    But the CƒÅrvƒÅkas said, "Hold on. That's a huge, unproven leap of faith."

    They argued that this kind of inference is completely unreliable and can't be trusted as a source of certain knowledge. Here‚Äôs their brilliant and devastating breakdown of why.

    For your inference "There is fire because there is smoke" to be 100% true, it has to rely on an unbreakable, universal rule. That rule is:

    "Wherever there is smoke, there is fire."

    This universal rule is the backbone of the inference. In Sanskrit, they call this rule VyƒÅpti (pronounced Vyaap-tee). VyƒÅpti means an invariable, universal relationship between two things.

    The CƒÅrvƒÅkas then asked a killer question: "How do you know that this VyƒÅpti is true? How can you be so sure that smoke is always accompanied by fire?"

    You might say, "Well, I've seen it a million times! I've seen fire and smoke together at campfires, in kitchens, in forest fires..."

    "Oh, so you've seen it a million times. But have you seen every single instance of smoke in the entire history of the universe‚Äîpast, present, and future?
